# Lesson Plan: The Life of Frederick Douglass (Pre–Civil War)
## Learning Objectives
- By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Describe key events in Frederick Douglass’s early life and escape from slavery.
- Explain how literacy shaped Douglass’s path toward abolitionist activism.
- Identify Douglass’s major contributions to the anti‑slavery movement before the Civil War.
- Analyze excerpts from Douglass’s writings to understand his perspective on slavery and freedom.

## Materials
- Excerpts from the Frederick Douglass reading (your attached document)
- Projector or whiteboard
- Student notebooks
- Optional: Audio/video of “What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?”

## Lesson Sequence
01 - Activate Prior KnowledgeAsk students what they already know about slavery, abolition, or Frederick Douglass to establish context.<BR>
02 - Introduce Douglass’s Early LifeProvide a brief overview of his childhood, separation from family, and early experiences with slavery.<BR>
03 - Explore Literacy and ResistanceGuide students through the section describing how Douglass learned to read and how this shaped his resistance.<BR>
04 - Trace His Escape JourneyWalk students through the timeline of his escape, highlighting Anna Murray’s role and the dangers he faced.<BR>
05 - Discuss Abolitionist WorkExamine Douglass’s speeches, writings, and activism, including the North Star and the Hundred Conventions tour.<BR>
06 - Analyze Primary QuotesHave students read short excerpts from Douglass’s letter to Thomas Auld and discuss tone and purpose.<BR>
07 - Reflect and SynthesizeStudents summarize what they learned and connect Douglass’s experiences to broader themes of justice and equality.<BR>

## Discussion Questions
- Use these to deepen understanding or as journal prompts:
- Why was learning to read such a powerful turning point for Douglass?<BR>
“Sophia… stopped teaching Douglass. She hid the reading materials from him.”
- How did Douglass’s personal experiences shape his views on slavery?
- What risks did Douglass and Anna Murray take during his escape?
- Why do you think Douglass’s autobiography had such a strong impact on the abolitionist movement?
- How does Douglass balance anger and compassion in his letter to Thomas Auld?<BR>
“There is no roof under which you would be more safe than mine…”

## Student Activities (Choose 1–2)
- Timeline Creation: Students build a visual timeline of Douglass’s life up to 1860.
- Quote Analysis: Students annotate a short excerpt from the Auld letter or the “London Reception Speech.”
- Map the Escape: Students trace Douglass’s escape route on a map and label key locations.

Mini‑Debate: “Was literacy the most important factor in Douglass’s journey to freedom?”

## Assessment Options
- Exit Ticket: One thing they learned, one question they still have.
- Short Paragraph: Explain how Douglass’s early life prepared him for his abolitionist work.
- Group Presentation: Summarize one major phase of Douglass’s life (early life, escape, activism).

## Extension Opportunities
- Listen to or watch a reading of “What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?”
- Compare Douglass’s narrative to another formerly enslaved person’s account.
- Research the Underground Railroad and Douglass’s role in assisting over 400 escapees.
