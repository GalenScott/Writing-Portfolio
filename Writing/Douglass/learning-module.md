# Frederick Douglass learning Module

This module is designed for placement in a GitHub /docs folder. It provides a structured, classroom-ready lesson plan based on the life and work of Frederick Douglass prior to the Civil War.

## Overview

This lesson introduces students to the early life, escape from slavery, and abolitionist work of Frederick Douglass. It includes objectives, materials, a teaching sequence, discussion questions, activities, and assessments.

## Learning Objectives

- Describe key events in Frederick Douglass’s early life and enslavement.
- Explain how literacy shaped Douglass’s path toward activism.
- Identify Douglass’s major contributions to the abolitionist movement.
- Analyze excerpts from Douglass’s writings to understand his perspective on slavery and freedom.

## Materials

- Excerpts from the Frederick Douglass reading
- Projector or whiteboard
- Student notebooks
- Optional: Video or audio of Douglass’s speeches

## Lesson Sequence

1. Warm-Up (5 minutes) <BR>
Students respond to a prompt: “Why might learning to read be considered an act of resistance?”
2. Introduction (5 minutes) <BR>
Brief overview of Frederick Douglass and his significance.
3. Direct Instruction (10 minutes) <BR>
- Teacher summarizes key events from Douglass’s early life, including:
  * Separation from his mother
  * Learning to read in Baltimore
  * Teaching other enslaved people
  * Confrontation with Edward Covey
  * Escape from slavery
4. Guided Reading (10 minutes)
- Students read selected excerpts from the provided document.
- Teacher highlights:
  * Douglass’s determination to learn
  * His reflections on slavery
  * His early activism
5. Discussion (10 minutes)
- Students discuss:
  * How literacy influenced Douglass’s worldview
  * The risks he took to escape slavery
  * Why his autobiography was so influential
6. Activity (10 minutes)
Choose one:
- Timeline Creation: Students create a timeline of Douglass’s life.
- Quote Analysis: Students annotate a short excerpt.
- Escape Route Map: Students map Douglass’s journey to freedom.
7. Assessment (5 minutes)
- Exit ticket: Students write one new fact they learned and one question they still have.

## Discussion Questions
- Why was learning to read such a powerful turning point for Douglass?
- How did Douglass’s personal experiences shape his activism?
- What risks did Douglass and Anna Murray take during his escape?
- Why do you think Douglass’s autobiography had such a strong impact?
- How does Douglass balance anger and compassion in his letter to Thomas Auld?

## Extension Opportunities
- Listen to or watch a reading of “What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?”
- Compare Douglass’s narrative to another formerly enslaved person’s account.
- Research Douglass’s work on the Underground Railroad.
