# Outline of Key Information Covered in the Frederick Douglass Document

## 1. Introduction to Frederick Douglass
-	Douglass as a major 19th century abolitionist, orator, and statesman.
-	His belief in equal rights for all people.
-	His antislavery writings made him famous.

## 2. Early Life & Enslavement
###  2.1 Birth & Family Background
-	Born Frederick Augustus Washington Bailey (likely February 1818).
-	Mixed Black and Native American heritage; father likely a white plantation owner.
-	Separated from his mother as an infant; raised by grandmother Betsy Bailey.
-	When Douglass was an infant, he was separated from his mother and placed with his grandmother, Betsy Bailey.

###  2.2 Childhood Under Slavery
-	Sent to Wye House Plantation at age six.
-	Transferred to Lucretia Auld after overseer Aaron Anthony died.
-	Moved frequently between owners.
2.3 Learning to Read & Write
-	Sent to Hugh and Sophia Auld in Baltimore at age eight.
-	Sophia began teaching him until pressured by anti education sentiment.
-	Douglass continued learning secretly from neighborhood children and found materials.

###  2.4 Early Acts of Resistance
-	Taught other enslaved people to read the New Testament while working for William Freeland.
-	Lessons violently shut down by slaveholders.
-	At age sixteen, fought back against Edward Covey, ending Covey’s beatings.

##  3. Escape From Slavery
###  3.1 Failed Attempts
-	First attempt in 1833; second in 1836 (resulted in jail).
###  3.2 Successful Escape (3 September 1838)
-	Aided by Anna Murray, a free Black woman who provided clothes, money, and papers.
-	Traveled by train and steamboat through Maryland, Delaware, and Pennsylvania.
-	Reached David Ruggles’ safe house in New York.

###  3.3 Marriage & Settlement
-	Married Anna Murray (15 September 1838).
-	Settled in New Bedford, MA; changed surname to Douglass.
-	Had five children; Rosetta and Charles later helped with his newspaper.
##  4. Abolitionist Involvement
###  4.1 Early Activism in New Bedford
-	Attended abolitionist meetings; first invited to speak.
-	Influenced by William Lloyd Garrison and The Liberator.
 
###  4.2 Expansion of Activism
-	Moved to Lynn, MA (1841).
-	Protested segregation after being kicked off a train.
-	Traveled widely across East and Midwest speaking against slavery.
###  4.3 The Hundred Conventions Project (1843)
-	Six month speaking tour with the American Anti Slavery Society.
-	Assaulted by a mob in Indiana; rescued by the Hardy family.

###  4.4 First Autobiography (1845)
-	Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave.
-	Sold 5,000 copies in four months; 30,000 by 1860.
##  5. Exile in Britain & Ireland (1845–1847)
-	Left U.S. due to fear of recapture after naming his owner in his book.
-	Delivered major speeches, including the “London Reception Speech.”
-	Met prominent abolitionists (O’Connell, Clarkson, Richardson family).
-	Anna and Ellen Richardson raised funds to legally purchase his freedom.
 
##  6. Return to the U.S. & Later Activism
###  6.1 Founding the North Star (1847)
-	Used British funds to start the newspaper.
-	Slogan: “Right is of no Sex—Truth is of no Color…”
-	Merged into The Frederick Douglass Paper in 1851.
###  6.2 Underground Railroad Work
-	Douglass and Anna helped more than 400 enslaved people escape.

###  6.3 Letter to Thomas Auld (1848)
-	Open letter condemning slavery and Auld’s actions.
-	Powerful emotional passages describing slavery’s horrors.
-	Ends with unexpected compassion.
 
##  7. Notable Pre–Civil War Achievements
-	1848: Attended Seneca Falls Convention.
-	1848: Elected President of the National Negro Convention.
-	1850s: Advocated for desegregated education.
-	1852: Delivered “What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?”
-	1853: Published The Heroic Slave.
-	1855: Published second autobiography My Bondage and My Freedom.
